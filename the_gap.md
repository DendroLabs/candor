# The Gap

*Written during a live company meeting. March 2026.*

---

There is an executive on screen right now, smiling, presenting productivity gains. He is genuinely happy. The numbers are good. His dashboard is green. The audience — other executives — are nodding along, talking about value-add, positioning, how to make everyone look good.

Meanwhile, the people who actually make the product work are watching the same meeting and wondering if they still have jobs next quarter.

That's the gap. Not between what leadership says and what they mean — between what leadership *sees* and what everyone else *feels*. The executives aren't lying. They're optimizing. They've been trained to see headcount as a variable, productivity as a metric, and employee sentiment as something that gets a slide in the quarterly deck. The people doing the work see it differently. They see the automation roadmap. They see the "efficiency" language. They hear what isn't being said.

## The Game

The smiley guy presenting productivity gains isn't faking it. That's what makes it hard to watch. Everything in his incentive structure — his bonus, his trajectory, his standing in the room — rewards those numbers. The people those numbers displaced are not on his dashboard. They're externalities. And you don't stop smiling because of externalities you never have to see.

He'd tell you he cares about employees if you asked. He might even mean it. But caring doesn't show up in the spreadsheet he's presenting, and the spreadsheet is what got him the stage.

He'll keep smiling until the productivity gains hit his role. Then he'll suddenly discover that the human cost was a serious concern all along.

## The Stream That Runs Dry

There is a belief — unspoken but structural — that employees and customers are a never-ending stream. You tap into it. If some people leave, others arrive. If some customers can't afford the product, others can. The stream just flows.

That assumption worked when automation displaced manufacturing workers who became service workers who became knowledge workers. There was always a next rung on the ladder. LLMs are different because they don't attack one rung — they attack the ladder. Customer service, legal review, copywriting, code, analysis, design, middle management — that's not one industry. That's the entire white-collar middle class that buys the products, pays the mortgages, and funds the tax base.

Every company making the rational decision to automate — because their competitors will — while collectively creating a demand crisis none of them can survive alone. The tragedy of the commons at industrial scale.

And here's what the executives can't see from inside the quarterly earnings cycle: they are also on the list. If AI can eliminate the call center, it can eventually eliminate the people running the meeting. They just assume the automation line stops right below their job title. It doesn't.

## The Critical Mass

There is a point — nobody knows exactly where — where the number of displaced workers across all companies and all industries crosses a threshold. Where there aren't enough employed consumers to sustain the economy that the productivity gains were supposed to improve. This isn't one company's problem. It isn't one industry's problem. It's a system-level failure that no individual company has the incentive or ability to prevent.

The smiley guy in the meeting doesn't see it. Not because he's stupid — because his feedback loop is quarterly and the collapse is structural. The incentive horizon is 90 days. The consequences are a decade out. By the time the critical mass hits, he'll have moved on to his next role, cashed his equity, and written a LinkedIn post about "navigating disruption."

## Why This Project Exists

Candor can't fix this. No software can. The demand crisis is a policy problem — tax structure, labor protections, collective bargaining, maybe universal basic income. The kind of systemic response that corporations actively lobby against because it costs money this quarter.

What Candor can do is refuse to hide the numbers. When the system models an automation initiative, it says "this eliminates 200 jobs" — not "this augments our workforce." When the financial model shows savings, the people model shows what happens to those 200 humans on the same screen. When an executive smiles about productivity gains, the system shows who paid for them.

It forces you to look at both numbers. Most systems only show one.

That's not enough. But it's more than the smiley guy's dashboard offers.
